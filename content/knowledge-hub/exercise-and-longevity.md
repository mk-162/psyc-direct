## The Single Best Predictor of Longevity

If you had to choose one metric that best predicts how long you'll live, it wouldn't be blood pressure, cholesterol, or even BMI. It would be **cardiorespiratory fitness** — specifically, your VO2 max.

A landmark 2018 study in JAMA Network Open found that low cardiorespiratory fitness was a stronger predictor of mortality than smoking, diabetes, or hypertension. People in the bottom 25% for fitness had roughly five times the mortality risk of those in the top 2.5%.

The good news: fitness is modifiable at any age.

## The Four Pillars of Exercise for Longevity

### 1. Zone 2 Cardio (The Foundation)

Zone 2 training — steady-state exercise where you can just about hold a conversation — is the backbone of metabolic health. It:

- Improves mitochondrial function
- Enhances fat oxidation
- Lowers resting heart rate
- Improves insulin sensitivity
- Builds the aerobic base for everything else

**Target:** 150–200 minutes per week (3–4 sessions of 45–60 minutes)

### 2. Strength Training (Non-Negotiable After 30)

Muscle mass declines approximately 3–8% per decade after 30, accelerating after 60. This isn't just about aesthetics — it's about:

- **Fall prevention** — the leading cause of injury-related death in over-65s
- **Metabolic health** — muscle is the largest glucose sink in the body
- **Bone density** — resistance training is the most effective intervention for osteoporosis prevention
- **Functional independence** — the ability to carry shopping, climb stairs, get up from the floor

**Target:** 2–3 sessions per week, focusing on compound movements (squat, hinge, push, pull, carry)

### 3. High-Intensity Interval Training (The Efficiency Play)

HIIT improves VO2 max more efficiently than steady-state cardio alone. Short bursts of high effort (80–90% max heart rate) followed by recovery periods:

- Improve cardiac output
- Enhance mitochondrial density
- Boost post-exercise calorie burn
- Take less time than Zone 2 sessions

**Target:** 1–2 sessions per week, 20–30 minutes including warm-up and cool-down

### 4. Stability and Mobility (The Forgotten Pillar)

Flexibility and balance work prevents injury, maintains joint health, and preserves the ability to move well:

- Yoga, Pilates, or dedicated mobility work
- Balance challenges (single-leg exercises)
- Hip and thoracic spine mobility

**Target:** Daily movement practice, even just 10 minutes

## The Minimum Effective Dose

Not everyone has 6–8 hours a week for exercise. The evidence suggests meaningful longevity benefits start at:

- **150 minutes of moderate activity per week** (about 22 minutes daily)
- **2 strength sessions per week** (can be as short as 30 minutes each)

That's roughly 3.5 hours per week. For the time investment, nothing else in medicine comes close to the return.

## When to Get Tested

A sports performance screening provides objective data on where you stand and where to focus:

- **VO2 max testing** — your cardiovascular ceiling
- **Body composition** — lean mass vs. fat mass (far more useful than BMI)
- **Resting metabolic rate** — how many calories you actually burn
- **Movement screening** — identifying restrictions or imbalances before they become injuries
- **Cardiac screening** — ECG and relevant blood markers for safe exercise
