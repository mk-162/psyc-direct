## When to Instruct a Psychiatrist vs Psychologist

### Instruct a Psychiatrist When:
- **Diagnosis of mental illness** is in question (psychosis, bipolar, severe depression)
- **Mental Health Act** assessment or hospital transfer recommendation needed
- **Medication and treatment** considerations are central to the case
- **Medical causation** of psychiatric symptoms needs establishing
- **Diminished responsibility** defense requires psychiatric evidence of abnormality of mind
- **Psychiatric risk** of serious harm needs medical assessment
- **Dual diagnosis** (mental illness + substance misuse) requiring medical formulation

### Instruct a Psychologist When:
- **Cognitive assessment** (IQ, neuropsychological testing) needed
- **Learning disability** or autism assessment required
- **Risk assessment** using structured tools (HCR-20, RSVP) is primary focus
- **Personality assessment** not requiring medical diagnosis
- **Psychological therapy** recommendations needed

### Instruct Both When:
- **Complex cases** requiring both diagnostic clarity (psychiatry) and psychological formulation
- **Diminished responsibility** cases benefiting from both medical and psychological evidence
- **Risk assessment** where mental illness and psychological factors both relevant

## Types of Psychiatric Assessments We Provide

### Parole Board Psychiatric Reports
Comprehensive psychiatric risk assessment for parole hearings, addressing:
- Current mental state and psychiatric diagnosis
- Psychiatric treatment history and compliance
- Mental illness as risk factor for violence or sexual offending
- Protective factors (medication, therapeutic engagement)
- Recommendations for release conditions and community psychiatric support

### Mental Health Act Assessments (s.47/s.48 Transfer)
Medical assessment for hospital transfer under Mental Health Act 1983, including:
- Diagnosis of mental disorder meeting MHA criteria
- Nature and degree of disorder requiring hospital treatment
- Treatability assessment
- Hospital suitability and bed availability liaison
- Recommendations to Ministry of Justice

### Pre-Sentencing Psychiatric Reports
Comprehensive psychiatric report for sentencing, including:
- Psychiatric diagnosis (ICD-11/DSM-5)
- Mental health history and treatment
- Causation and timing of mental illness in relation to offense
- Treatability and prognosis with/without treatment
- Sentencing recommendations (hospital order, community psychiatric treatment, etc.)

### Diminished Responsibility Psychiatric Evidence
Specialist psychiatric assessment for s.2 Homicide Act defense, addressing:
- Abnormality of mental functioning at time of killing
- Recognized medical condition (psychiatric diagnosis)
- Medical evidence of substantial impairment
- Causal link between condition and killing

### Dangerous and Severe Personality Disorder (DSPD)
Specialist assessment of severe personality disorder, including:
- Diagnostic formulation (ICD-11 PD categories)
- Risk assessment in context of personality pathology
- Treatability and engagement prospects
- Management recommendations (DSPD unit, therapeutic community, etc.)

### Risk of Serious Harm (Psychiatric)
Medical assessment of psychiatric risk factors, including:
- Psychotic symptoms increasing violence risk
- Command hallucinations and delusional beliefs
- Impulsivity and emotional dysregulation
- Suicidal and self-harm risk
- Substance-induced states and dual diagnosis

### Fitness for Trial (Psychiatric)
Psychiatric assessment of mental illness impact on trial fitness, including:
- Psychotic symptoms affecting understanding or participation
- Severe depression affecting engagement
- Mania or acute mental illness interfering with court process
- Treatability and prospects for restoration of fitness

### Medication and Treatment Compliance
Assessment of psychiatric medication history, including:
- Current medication regime and compliance
- Historical treatment response
- Side effects and medication refusal
- Recommendations for optimal psychiatric treatment in custody or community

## FAQs

**Q: What is the difference between a psychiatrist and psychologist for prison law cases?**
A: **Psychiatrists** are medical doctors (GMC-registered) who can diagnose mental illness, prescribe medication, and provide medical opinions on mental disorders. **Psychologists** (HCPC-registered) provide psychological assessment, cognitive testing, and psychological formulation. For cases involving mental illness diagnosis or medication, psychiatric input is often essential. For IQ testing or psychological risk assessment, psychology may be more appropriate. Complex cases may benefit from both.

**Q: Are your psychiatrists Section 12 approved?**
A: Yes. Where Mental Health Act assessments are required, we ensure the psychiatrist is Section 12 approved. All our forensic psychiatrists are GMC-registered Consultants with experience in secure settings.

**Q: Can your psychiatrists assess prisoners in custody?**
A: Yes. Our psychiatrists conduct medical examinations in prisons, remand centers, and secure hospitals. They hold appropriate security clearances and are experienced in prison healthcare liaison.

**Q: Do you work at Legal Aid rates for psychiatric reports?**
A: Yes. Our psychiatrists accept Legal Aid Agency (LAA) rates for both defense and prosecution psychiatric work. We can provide transparent fixed-fee quotes within 24 hours.

**Q: What are typical turnaround times for prison law psychiatric reports?**
A: Standard reports are delivered within **4-6 weeks** from instruction. **Fast-track options** are available for urgent parole hearings or Mental Health Act assessments â€” we can deliver reports in 1-2 weeks when required.

**Q: Can your psychiatrists recommend hospital transfer under the Mental Health Act?**
A: Yes. Our psychiatrists conduct s.47/s.48 assessments and liaise with secure hospitals regarding bed availability. They can provide the medical recommendations required for Ministry of Justice approval of transfer.

**Q: What diagnostic frameworks do your psychiatrists use?**
A: Our psychiatrists use **ICD-11** (World Health Organization) and **DSM-5** (American Psychiatric Association) diagnostic criteria. Reports clearly reference diagnostic codes and criteria met.
