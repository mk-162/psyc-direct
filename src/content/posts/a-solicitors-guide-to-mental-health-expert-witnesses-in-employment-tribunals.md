## **Choosing the Right Expert Psychology vs Psychiatry**

One of the most crucial decisions in employment tribunal cases involving mental health is selecting the appropriate type of expert witness. While both [**psychology and psychiatry experts**](https://www.psychologydirect.co.uk/news/psychologist-vs-psychiatrist-for-an-expert-witness-report-choosing-the-right-expert/) can provide valuable evidence, they serve different purposes and offer distinct types of expertise.

### **When You Need a Psychiatry Expert Witness**

Psychiatrists are medical doctors specialising in mental health conditions. In employment cases, psychiatry experts are essential when you need medical diagnosis, treatment recommendations, or assessment of severe psychiatric conditions. They become indispensable in several scenarios:

- When employees present with formal psychiatric conditions such as major depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, bipolar disorder, ADHD, or autism spectrum disorders, a psychiatrist can provide definitive medical diagnosis and evidence-based treatment recommendations. This medical opinion carries significant weight in tribunals when establishing the severity and legitimacy of mental health claims.
- Cases involving the impact of psychiatric medications on work performance, side effects affecting employment capacity, or disputes over reasonable adjustments related to medication schedules require psychiatric expertise. Only psychiatrists can provide authoritative opinions on medication effects and necessary accommodations.
- Complex cases involving serious psychiatric conditions with significant functional impairment need psychiatric assessment to establish the medical nature and severity of the condition. This includes psychotic disorders, severe mood disorders, and complex trauma presentations.
- When employers question whether severe mental health conditions render an employee medically unfit for their role, psychiatric assessment provides the clinical medical opinion tribunals require. This is particularly crucial in safety-critical roles or positions requiring high-level cognitive function.
- Claims involving psychiatric injury (as opposed to psychological distress) require psychiatric experts to establish medical causation, prognosis, and treatment needs. The distinction between psychiatric injury and psychological distress can significantly impact case outcomes and compensation levels.

### **When Psychology Experts Are Most Appropriate**

Psychologists specialise in human behaviour, cognitive function, and psychological wellbeing. In employment contexts, they excel at:

- Evaluating how psychological factors impact specific work-related abilities and identifying practical accommodations. Psychologists are particularly skilled at breaking down complex psychological presentations into concrete workplace implications.
- Assessing the psychological effects of workplace experiences without necessarily requiring formal psychiatric diagnosis. This is valuable in cases where the impact is significant but doesn't meet the threshold for psychiatric disorder.
- Examining cognitive abilities, learning differences, and their workplace implications. This includes assessment of attention, memory, executive function, and processing speed as they relate to job performance.
- Providing detailed, evidence-based suggestions for reasonable adjustments based on psychological assessment. 

## **When Mental Health Experts Add Value**

Employment tribunals increasingly recognise that workplace mental health conditions require the same rigorous professional assessment as physical injuries. The difference is that mental health workplace issues often lack visible symptoms, making expert evaluation crucial for establishing causation, severity, and long-term impact.

Mental health expert witnesses become essential in several key scenarios:

### **Work-Related Stress and Mental Health Claims**

When employees experience anxiety, depression, or stress-related conditions they believe stem from their workplace, tribunals require evidence that goes well beyond GP records or occupational health referrals. Qualified experts can provide objective assessment of whether the workplace environment genuinely caused or significantly contributed to the mental health condition.

This isn't about questioning the validity of an employee's experience, it's about providing the tribunal with the professional analysis needed to understand the complex interplay between workplace factors, individual vulnerability, and psychological outcomes.

### **Fitness for Work Disputes**

These cases arise when employers question an employee's capacity to perform their role safely. Rather than simple diagnosis, these situations require careful assessment of functional capacity and potential for accommodation. Expert witnesses can evaluate whether reasonable adjustments might enable continued employment, providing evidence-based recommendations that benefit both parties.

### **Discrimination and Mental Health**

Claims involving harassment, bullying, or failure to make reasonable adjustments often require expert evidence to establish the psychological or psychiatric impact of discriminatory treatment. These assessments help tribunals understand how workplace experiences have affected an individual's mental health and overall functioning.

## **Reasonable Adjustments and Mental Health Conditions**

The **[Equality Act 2010](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/15/contents)** places positive duties on employers to make reasonable adjustments for employees whose mental health conditions constitute disabilities. Expert witnesses provide evidence-based opinions about effective accommodations.

### **Assessment Considerations Include:**

- The specific nature of psychological or psychiatric symptoms and workplace impact
- Evidence-based adjustments that address identified functional limitations
- Likelihood of adjustment effectiveness
- Consideration of symptom fluctuation and flexibility needs

### **Common Effective Adjustments:**

- Modified working patterns to accommodate treatment schedules or medication timing
- Adjusted workload during periods of increased symptoms
- Environmental modifications to reduce specific anxiety triggers or sensory sensitivities
- Enhanced supervision and support during challenging periods
- Structured phased return-to-work programmes following treatment or crisis

The crucial legal question is whether adjustments are "reasonable" in terms of both effectiveness for the individual and practicality for the employer. Expert witnesses provide professional opinions on both aspects, often drawing from research evidence and clinical experience.

## **Relevant Case Law and Legal Precedents**

Understanding key precedents helps solicitors maximise the impact of expert evidence:

[**Melville v Home Office** [2005]](https://oldsquare.co.uk/melville-v-home-office/): Established employer liability for psychiatric injury where clear stress warnings were ignored. Expert evidence was crucial in demonstrating foreseeability and employer failure to act appropriately.

[**Intel Corporation (UK) Ltd v Daw** [2007]](https://www.casemine.com/judgement/uk/5a8ff71760d03e7f57ea767b): Clarified that stress claims require proof of "material contribution" from workplace factors rather than sole causation. Experts must carefully analyse competing causes and their relative significance.

[**Hartman v South Essex Mental Health NHS Trust** [2005]](https://www.casemine.com/judgement/uk/5a8ff7b060d03e7f57eb1429): Emphasised the importance of expert evidence in establishing both psychiatric injury existence and its life impact.

### **ADHD and Autism in Employment Contexts**

Employment cases involving ADHD and autism spectrum disorders present unique challenges that often require specialised psychiatric assessment:

### **ADHD in the Workplace**

ADHD can significantly impact workplace performance through difficulties with attention, organisation, time management, and impulse control. Psychiatric assessment is essential for:

- Confirming diagnosis and ruling out other conditions
- Assessing medication effects and optimal treatment
- Identifying specific workplace impacts and accommodation needs
- Distinguishing between ADHD symptoms and performance issues

### **Autism Spectrum Disorders**

Autism in employment contexts often involves challenges with social communication, sensory sensitivities, and routine changes. Psychiatric expertise helps establish:

- Accurate diagnosis and assessment of support needs
- Sensory and environmental accommodation requirements
- Social communication support strategies
- Reasonable adjustment recommendations
