## **ADHD in Family Law**

In family law, ADHD in a parent or a child can introduce significant complexities, particularly concerning **parental capacity assessments** and **child welfare considerations**.

### **Impacts to consider in family law:**

- A parent with undiagnosed or unmanaged ADHD might struggle with organisation, consistency, emotional regulation, and follow-through – all crucial aspects of effective parenting. This isn't a judgment on their love for their child, but a recognition of the specific challenges ADHD can present. An expert assessment can distinguish between wilful neglect and difficulties arising from executive dysfunction, offering recommendations for support rather than just criticism.
- For a child with ADHD, expert input can clarify their specific educational and developmental needs, ensuring that court orders or care plans adequately address these. This is essential for their long-term well-being and progress.
- ADHD can also impact communication and conflict resolution within families, which is highly relevant in divorce or separation proceedings.

An expert can provide a detailed assessment of how ADHD symptoms specifically impact a parent's ability to meet a child's needs or engage with processes, offering balanced insights and actionable recommendations for support, rather than simply identifying deficits. This can be vital for making fair and child-focused decisions.

## **ADHD in Criminal Law**

In criminal law, ADHD can play a significant, though often unrecognised, role in offending behaviour and the subsequent legal process.

### **Impacts to consider in criminal law:**

- Core symptoms of ADHD – particularly impulsivity and difficulty with planning and foreseeing consequences – can contribute to actions that lead to criminal charges. This is not to excuse behaviour but to provide context for it.
- Individuals with ADHD may struggle with attention during court proceedings, remembering instructions, or accurately recounting events, potentially impacting their ability to participate effectively in their own defence.
- An expert assessment can provide valuable mitigation evidence, explaining how ADHD symptoms contributed to the offence, demonstrating a capacity for rehabilitation if the condition is managed, and informing appropriate sentencing that includes treatment or support. This can lead to more proportionate and rehabilitative outcomes.
- Individuals with ADHD may also be more vulnerable to exploitation or coercion, which is highly relevant in certain criminal cases.

A psychologist can assess the presence and severity of ADHD, establish how the condition may have influenced the alleged offence, and provide recommendations for intervention or support. This contextual understanding is critical for presenting a comprehensive defence or mitigation plea.
